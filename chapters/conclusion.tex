\section{Summary}

\paragraph{Inception Based Metrics and HPSv3}
The first step of this thesis was to give the theoretical foundation for inception based metrics used to both filter and evaluate filtering metrics. Doing so lead us to identify several problems with the FID, Precision, Recall and Rarity  metrics, which motivated the development of the Human Perception Score \acs{HPS} Metric. This new metric is aligned with human perception (as the name suggests), independent from all other metrics, not affected by an artificial inflation effect when reducing the number of samples and therefore also does not require huge batches of samples to be computed.

\paragraph{Foundations for Bayesian Generative Uncertainty}
We then started to lay the foundation for Bayesian Generative Uncertainty (\acs{GU}). To do that we first defined what diffusion models are and what training objective they optimize. We then briefly explained what Bayesian machine learning was and the Laplace Approximation \acs{LA} used to make the Bayesian inference tractable. While doing so, we identified a problem with how diffusion models used in this thesis are trained and how it affects the \acs{LA} by exposing an error term that was directly proportional to the prior precision $\gamma$.

% \subsection{The Encoder, Not the Posterior}

% Throughout this thesis we operated within the Bayesian framing of \cite{jazbec_generative_2025}: sample models from the posterior, generate images deterministically under a fixed noise seed, embed them in a semantic space, and measure the resulting spread via entropy. The stated goal was to capture the diffusion model's \textit{epistemic uncertainty}, its lack of confidence in its learned weights, and turn it into a per-image quality score. Our direct noise injection (\acs{DNI}) experiment (\autoref{ch:semantic-encoder}) challenges that interpretation at its root.

% Replacing the Laplace posterior entirely with simple isotropic pixel-space noise produced near-identical filtering performance (\acs{AURC}: \acs{LLLA} $= 12.84$ vs.\ \acs{DNI} $= 12.82$). The formalization in \autoref{sec:formalize_dni_method} explains why. Under a first-order Taylor expansion of the encoder, the per-image uncertainty is proportional to the logarithmic sum of the squared Jacobian norms of the semantic encoder (\autoref{eq:jacobian_uncertainty}). High-quality images that lie on the encoder's training manifold sit in locally Lipschitz-stable regions, so small perturbations, whether drawn from the \acs{LLLA} posterior or from \acs{DNI}, barely move the embedding and yield low entropy. Failed images fall off the manifold, where the encoder becomes highly non-linear, its gradient norms explode, and the entropy spikes \parencite{novak_sensitivity_2018}.

% The implication is that \acs{GU} is better understood as an \textit{encoder defined off-manifold detector} than as a measure of the diffusion model's posterior spread. The Bayesian machinery supplies the perturbation, but it is the semantic encoder that decides what ``uncertain'' means, governed entirely by its own training manifold. The term ``Generative Uncertainty'' still names something real, the instability of an image in the encoder's semantic space, but that instability is not the diffusion model's epistemic uncertainty in the Bayesian sense.

% \subsection{The LLLA Paradox}

% This reframing also resolves an apparent contradiction that runs through the thesis. \autoref{ch:toy_exp_posterior} shows that the \acs{LLLA}, once properly stabilized through a high prior (the \acs{ICLA} limit) or temperature scaling, filters hallucinations very effectively on the Toy model, yet \autoref{ch:semantic-encoder} shows it is practically interchangeable with random noise on the \acs{ADM}. The two results stop conflicting once the encoder is taken into account.

% In the Toy experiment, uncertainty is computed directly in the two-dimensional output space, with no encoder mediating the signal. Weight perturbations from the \acs{LLLA} translate into genuine geometric displacements of the output, and the entropy reflects real variability in what the model produces. There, the quality of the posterior approximation genuinely shapes what gets filtered. In the \acs{ADM} experiment, the \acs{CLIP} encoder sits between the model output and the uncertainty score. The tiny last-layer weight perturbations from the \acs{LLLA} reach the encoder as pixel-level changes that are functionally indistinguishable from \acs{DNI}. The information bottleneck has moved from the model's weight space to the encoder's local sensitivity profile, and the \acs{LLLA}'s curvature information is further diluted by its own heavy approximations (diagonal Fisher, last layer only, and a Hessian estimated from just $1\%$ of the ImageNet training set) before it ever arrives. A curvature estimate built from so thin a slice of data is closer to a local noise floor around the pre-trained weights than to a global map of epistemic uncertainty.

% The Pearson correlation $r = 0.532$ between \acs{LLLA} and \acs{DNI} scores (\autoref{fig:la_role_corr}) supports a nuanced reading. It is high enough to rule out pure noise, so a weak posterior signal does survive the encoder, yet low enough that the two methods are not simply copies of each other. On aggregate statistics (\acs{FPR} and \acs{AURC}) they rank images similarly enough that the practical difference vanishes.

% \subsection{Two Regimes of Uncertainty: Deep Ensembles and the Laplace Approximation}

% The Toy experiments also expose a structural difference between the two posterior approximations that extends well beyond the controlled setting. Deep Ensembles (\acs{DE}), trained from independent random seeds, settle into different basins of the loss landscape. When a fixed noise seed is passed through every member, some members may assign the output to one Gaussian mode and others to a different one, producing high uncertainty for samples near the boundary between modes even when each individual model generates a perfectly valid, in-distribution sample (\autoref{sec:toy_what_gu_filter}). \acs{DE} therefore captures a broad notion of epistemic uncertainty that includes both geometric hallucinations and \textit{mode-assignment ambiguity}.

% The \acs{LLLA}, anchored to a single minimum, cannot reproduce this inter-basin disagreement. By jittering weights within the current basin it instead targets samples whose neighborhood induces large output displacements, that is, the geometric hallucinations and hard out-of-distribution cases. Our ablations show that the stabilized \acs{LLLA} removes hallucinations earlier in the rejection curve than \acs{DE}, before \acs{DE} catches up through its broader coverage. The choice between them is therefore application-dependent: for removing obvious visual artifacts the \acs{LLLA} is the more targeted and cheaper tool, whereas safety-critical settings such as medical image generation, where a model hesitating between two plausible but clinically distinct interpretations is itself the risk, call for \acs{DE}-style coverage. The MoLA framework \parencite{eschenhagen_mixtures_2021}, which combines \acs{DE} basin diversity with \acs{LLLA} intra-basin depth, offers a principled way to capture both regimes within a single estimator.

% \subsection{Encoder--Evaluation Alignment}

% A practical design principle follows from comparing the \acs{CLIP} encoder with the internal \acs{ADM} U-Net encoder (\autoref{ch:using_internal_encoder}). The internal encoder, trained only on ImageNet $128\times128$, retains lower FID and higher Recall through filtering because it is calibrated to what the \acs{ADM} has and has not learned. \acs{CLIP}, trained on a much broader human-aligned corpus, wins on the \acs{HPSv3} \acs{AURC} because its manifold is closer to that of the HPSv3 model. Three manifolds are in play at once: the generator's training data, the uncertainty encoder's training data, and the evaluation metric's training data, and the method performs best on whichever criterion those manifolds align with. The same logic explains retroactively why Realism and Rarity sometimes beat \acs{GU} on \acs{FPR} metrics in \autoref{ch:reproduce_adm}: they share the \texttt{Inception-v3} feature space with the evaluation itself (\autoref{sub:problems_inception}). Choosing an encoder aligned with both the generator and the target criterion is thus a first-class design decision, not an afterthought, and the principle generalizes to any uncertainty framework that routes model outputs through a fixed external representation.

% \paragraph{What this measures, and what it does not.}
% It is worth being precise about the trade-off. A properly specified Bayesian posterior, or a Deep Ensemble, measures the model's epistemic uncertainty in a well-established and interpretable way, and would almost certainly be more informative than \acs{DNI}. Its cost, however, makes it impractical outside critical applications such as medical imaging. \acs{DNI} sits at the opposite end of that spectrum. As formalized in \autoref{sec:formalize_dni_method}, it does not probe the weight-space posterior at all; it rests on the assumption that the Jacobian of the chosen layer grows for out-of-distribution inputs, so that off-manifold samples land in locally unstable regions of the representation. Where that assumption holds, the resulting score is an effective probe of whether a generated image, text, or other output lies far from the manifold the model was trained on. Whether a stronger posterior approximation, such as FLARE \parencite{gupta_quantifying_2026} or the Riemannian approach of \parencite{gegenfurtner_reducing_2026}, could produce a signal strong enough to survive the encoder bottleneck and add value beyond this cheap baseline remains, to us, the most compelling open question.

% This thesis began by taking the Bayesian framing of Generative Uncertainty at face value and ended by showing that, on the \acs{ADM}, the heavily approximated posterior was largely interchangeable with simple noise once it passed through an external encoder. We read that not as a negative result but as a redirection. The signal that mattered came from the geometry of an encoder, and that geometry is already present inside the generative model itself. Turning it into a cheap, model-intrinsic, and potentially architecture-agnostic probe of trustworthiness is, we believe, the most practical and far-reaching continuation of this work.
