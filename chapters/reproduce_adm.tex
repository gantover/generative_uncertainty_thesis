\chapter{Reproducing the ADM Experiment}
\label{ch:reproduce_adm}

The following experiment was originally conducted in \cite{jazbec_generative_2025} to prove the applicability and usefulness of \acs{GU} in a large scale, \acs{SOTA} diffusion model like \acs{ADM} against other baselines on the \acs{FPR} metrics. The idea here will be to prove that we can reproduce their experiment and get the same results. That will allow us to have the proper foundation to start extending their framework and conducting new experiments.

\section{Setup}

\paragraph{Pipeline}
The idea here is to first compute \acs{GU} as described in \autoref{ch:gu_theory} with $\mathrm{M}+1=6$ models (base model and 5 \acs{LLLA} sampled models) and create a dataset of $12032$ generated and scored images. 

% The idea here is to take the $\mathrm{M}+1=6$ ADM model weights including the base model and the 5 \acs{LLLA} sampled models, use a dataset of $12032$ noise samples through those $6$ models to get $12032$ generated images for each model. 

The objective is then to compare \acs{GU} to other baselines filtering metrics (\emph{Random}, \emph{Rarity}, \emph{Realism}, \autoref{sec:inception_metrics}).
Once we have the $(\mathrm{M}+1) \times 12032$ images, we evaluate the baselines filtering metrics on each of the $12032$ images. We then filter out chunks of the $12032$ images with respect to each filtering metric (taking out the images that perform worse on those filtering metrics first). The effect of that filtering is then measured on the \acs{FPR} metrics. 

% We measure the effect of filtering of by taking subset of $11000, 10000,..., 6000$ images and measuring the \acs{FPR} for each.

\paragraph{Implementation}
Thankfully, the original experiment from \cite{jazbec_generative_2025} was mostly already in their \texttt{github}. Although the \emph{Realism} baseline was already there, it was not fully implemented in the experiment's pipeline, the \emph{Rarity} baseline was not implemented at all. Some modifications were therefore made and are available in the \texttt{GitHub} of this thesis.

\paragraph{Confidence Intervals}
It is also worth noting that our reproduction of the \parencite{jazbec_generative_2025} experiment did not include confidence intervals. Doing so would necessitate running the very expensive\footnote{A full pass of the $\mathrm{M}+1=6$ models generating $12032$ images each takes around 20 hours on a DTU HPC \texttt{Tesla A100}} image generation pipeline multiple times. 
We see on \autoref{fig:jazbec_exp} that the confidence intervals stay relatively small anyway and are not strictly required to see if a method performs better than another one. For this experiment in particular, we only needed to verify that our results were compatible with theirs.

\subsection{Problems with the Inception Metrics}
\label{sub:show_n_inception_problem}

As teased in \autoref{sub:problems_inception}, we now address the effect of reducing $N$. To isolate this effect, we can look at the \texttt{random} baseline which randomly and progressively removes samples from $\boldsymbol\Phi_g$. In theory it should stay flat when reducing $N$, but we can clearly see the \acs{FID} and \emph{Recall} metrics rising for the \texttt{random} baseline.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=red!10!white,colframe=red!50!black,sharp corners]
For the remainder of the thesis, before interpreting the results of any filtering method, we will have to take into account the artificial rise in \acs{FID} and \emph{Recall} when reducing $N$ by looking at the \texttt{random} baseline first and correcting the results of the filtering method by how much the \texttt{random} baseline has risen.
\warning We don't believe we can truly isolate the effect of reducing $N$ this way, especially with such a small dataset to begin with, but we believe this gives us a rough idea.
\end{tcolorbox}
