\section{Interpretation for Precision and Recall Inflation Phenomenon}
\label{app:fpr_inflation}

\paragraph{Recall more affected than Precision}
One plausible explanation for this stems from the spatial differences between $\mathcal{M}_k(\boldsymbol\Phi_r)$ and $\mathcal{M}_k(\boldsymbol\Phi_g)$. 

\emph{Recall} evaluates whether real images fall within the generated manifold.
Generative models generally cover a smaller fraction of the true data distribution\footnote{This is also a motivation for the creation of metrics such as Precision and Recall}. Because generated samples are packed into a smaller subset of the feature space, lowering the sample size forces the $k$-NN boundaries of these dense clusters to grow. Since this generated manifold sits within the broader real distribution, expanding its boundaries rapidly engulfs peripheral real points, causing \emph{Recall} to spike artificially. 

In contrast, \emph{Precision} evaluates whether generated images fall within the real manifold. Because models tend to generate samples in safe, well-represented regions, most generated points already reside deep inside the real distribution. Therefore, when the broad real manifold expands outward into empty space due to sparsity, it captures very few new generated points, resulting in a much flatter growth curve for \emph{Precision}.

\paragraph{Asymmetry in sample sizes}
In \cite{jazbec_generative_2025}, we fix the size of the real dataset $|\boldsymbol{\Phi}_r| = 50{,}000$ and vary the size of the generated dataset $N = |\boldsymbol\Phi_f| \in [6000,\dots,12000]$. This asymmetry makes \emph{Precision} even less sensitive to $N$ because $\mathcal{M}_k(\boldsymbol\Phi_r)$ is fixed: only the number of generated points changes and most of them reside in the real distribution. \emph{Recall} however grows even more from it because it measures how well the huge $50{,}000$-point dataset fits into $\mathcal{M}_k(\boldsymbol\Phi_g)$, whose hyperspheres keep growing around the clusters.

\section{Mathematical Foundations of Diffusion Models}
\label{app:diffusion_math}

% \vspace{1cm}

This appendix provides the exhaustive mathematical proofs and derivations for the discrete-time diffusion framework outlined conceptually in \autoref{sec:bg_diffusion}. The forward-process and variational derivations follow \textcite{sohl2015thermo} and \textcite{ho_denoising_2020}, and the generalized sampler follows \textcite{song_denoising_2022}.

\subsection{The Forward Process and Reparameterization}
\label{app:diffusion_forward}

Following \textcite{ho_denoising_2020}, the single-step forward transition is defined as a Gaussian distribution centered around the scaled previous timestep:
\begin{equation}
    q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{1 - \beta_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_t I).
\end{equation}

Using standard properties of Gaussian distributions, we can express a sample $x_t$ via the reparameterization trick as:
\begin{equation}
    x_t = \sqrt{1-\beta_t}x_{t-1} + \sqrt{\beta_t}\epsilon_{t-1},
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{t-1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$. 

By recursively applying the reparameterization trick and leveraging the property that the sum of two independent Gaussian variables $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_1^2 I)$ and $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_2^2 I)$ results in a new Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(0, (\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2)I)$, we can merge the independent noise components at each step. Let $\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$ and $\overline{\alpha}_{t} = \prod_{s=1}^{t}\alpha_{s}$. The equation expands as follows:
\begin{align}
    x_t &= \sqrt{\alpha_t}x_{t-1} + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\epsilon_{t-1} \nonumber \\
        &= \sqrt{\alpha_t}(\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}x_{t-2} + \sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}}\epsilon_{t-2}) + \sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\epsilon_{t-1} \nonumber \\
        &= \sqrt{\alpha_t \alpha_{t-1}}x_{t-2} + \sqrt{\alpha_t(1-\alpha_{t-1}) + (1-\alpha_t)}\bar{\epsilon} \nonumber \\
        &= \dots \nonumber \\
        &= \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t}}x_{0} + \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}\epsilon.
\end{align}
Equivalently, the marginal is $q(x_t | x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}\,x_0, (1-\overline{\alpha}_t) I)$, which allows us to sample $x_t$ directly from $x_0$ without iterating through intermediate steps \parencite{ho_denoising_2020}.

\subsection{Derivation of the Variational Lower Bound (VLB)}
\label{app:diffusion_vlb}

This derivation follows \textcite{sohl2015thermo} and \textcite{ho_denoising_2020}. To optimize the neural network $p_\theta$, we must construct a tractable upper bound on the negative log-likelihood of the data. By introducing the forward process $q(x_{1:T}|x_0)$ and applying Jensen's inequality, we obtain the VLB:
\begin{align}
    -\log p_\theta(x_0) &= -\log \mathbb{E}_{q(x_{1:T}|x_0)} \left[ \frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T}|x_0)} \right] \nonumber \\
    &\le \mathbb{E}_{q(x_{1:T}|x_0)} \left[ -\log \frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T}|x_0)} \right] = \mathcal{L}_{VLB}.
\end{align}

We decompose this bound by expanding the joint distributions according to their Markov properties:
\begin{align}
    p_\theta(x_{0:T}) &= p(x_T) \prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_{t-1}|x_t), \\
    q(x_{1:T}|x_0) &= \prod_{t=1}^T q(x_t|x_{t-1}).
\end{align}

Substituting these into the bound yields:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{VLB} = \mathbb{E}_q \left[ -\log p(x_T) - \sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(x_{t-1}|x_t) + \sum_{t=1}^T \log q(x_t|x_{t-1}) \right].
\end{equation}

We resolve the intractable forward transition term $q(x_t|x_{t-1})$ by applying Bayes' rule conditioned on $x_0$:
\begin{equation}
    q(x_t|x_{t-1}) = \frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) q(x_t|x_0)}{q(x_{t-1}|x_0)}.
\end{equation}

Substituting this into the forward process summation and isolating the $t=1$ term gives:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{t=1}^T \log q(x_t|x_{t-1}) = \log q(x_1|x_0) + \sum_{t=2}^T \log \left( \frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) q(x_t|x_0)}{q(x_{t-1}|x_0)} \right).
\end{equation}

Expanding the logarithm inside the sum produces a telescoping series:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{t=2}^T \log q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) + \sum_{t=2}^T \log q(x_t|x_0) - \sum_{t=2}^T \log q(x_{t-1}|x_0).
\end{equation}

The last two terms collapse to $\log q(x_T|x_0) - \log q(x_1|x_0)$. Recombining this with the isolated $t=1$ term yields a simplified sum:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{t=1}^T \log q(x_t|x_{t-1}) = \sum_{t=2}^T \log q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) + \log q(x_T|x_0).
\end{equation}

Substituting this back into the full $\mathcal{L}_{VLB}$ equation allows us to group the terms into mathematically elegant Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergences:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{VLB} = \mathbb{E}_q \left[ \log \frac{q(x_T|x_0)}{p(x_T)} + \sum_{t=2}^T \log \frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{t-1}|x_t)} - \log p_\theta(x_0|x_1) \right].
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{VLB} = \mathbb{E}_q \left[ L_T + \sum_{t=2}^T L_{t-1} + L_0 \right],
\end{equation}
where the three groups of terms are
\begin{align}
    L_T &= D_{KL}(q(x_T | x_0) \parallel p(x_T)), \\
    L_{t-1} &= D_{KL}(q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0) \parallel p_\theta(x_{t-1} | x_t)), \\
    L_0 &= -\log p_\theta(x_0 | x_1).
\end{align}
The prior-matching term $L_T$ carries no learnable parameters (the forward process is fixed), and is therefore constant during training. This is the variance-reduced decomposition of \textcite{ho_denoising_2020}, in which every KL term compares two Gaussians and can be evaluated in closed form.

\subsection{Analytical Solution of the KL Divergence}
\label{app:diffusion_kl}

When conditioned on the original uncorrupted image $x_0$, the true reverse step $q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0)$ within $L_{t-1}$ becomes a tractable Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(x_{t-1}; \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0), \tilde{\beta}_t I)$. The analytical mean and variance of this distribution are derived as \parencite{ho_denoising_2020}:
\begin{align}
    \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) &= \frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}\beta_t}{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}x_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}x_t, \\
    \tilde{\beta}_t &= \frac{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}\beta_t.
\end{align}

Assuming our network $p_\theta$ is parameterized as a Gaussian with fixed variance $\Sigma_t = \sigma_t^2 I$ and a learned mean $\mu_\theta(x_t, t)$, the KL divergence between the two Gaussians reduces (up to a $\theta$-independent constant $C$ absorbing the covariance terms) to the expected Mean Squared Error (MSE) between their means \parencite{ho_denoising_2020}:
\begin{equation}
    L_{t-1} = \mathbb{E}_q \left[ \frac{1}{2\sigma_t^2} ||\tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) - \mu_\theta(x_t, t)||_2^2 \right] + C.
\end{equation}

By expressing $x_0$ algebraically in terms of $x_t$ and the noise $\epsilon$ via \autoref{eq:forward_reparam},
\begin{equation}
    x_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}}(x_t - \sqrt{1 - \overline{\alpha}_t}\epsilon),
\end{equation}
and substituting this into the target mean $\tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0)$ defined above, the dependence on $x_0$ cancels and $\tilde{\mu}_t$ is left entirely as a function of $x_t$ and the injected noise $\epsilon$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_target_mean_noise}
    \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}}\,\epsilon \right).
\end{equation}
Since $x_t$ is supplied to the network as input, the only unknown quantity it must recover is $\epsilon$. Following \textcite{ho_denoising_2020}, we therefore parameterize the model's mean to mirror \autoref{eq:app_target_mean_noise} exactly, replacing the true noise $\epsilon$ with a noise-prediction network $\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_mu_theta}
    \mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}} \left( x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}} \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \right).
\end{equation}
With this choice the mean error in $L_{t-1}$ becomes a pure noise-prediction error, since
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mu}_t(x_t, x_0) - \mu_\theta(x_t, t) = \frac{\beta_t}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}}\big(\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) - \epsilon\big).
\end{equation}
Substituting this back into $L_{t-1}$ simplifies the exact KL divergence into the weighted MSE loss of \textcite{ho_denoising_2020}:
\begin{equation}
    L_{t-1} - C = \mathbb{E}_{x_0, \epsilon} \left[ \frac{\beta_t^2}{2\sigma_t^2 \alpha_t (1-\overline{\alpha}_t)} ||\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}x_0 + \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}\epsilon, t)||_2^2 \right].
\end{equation}
As noted in \autoref{sec:bg_diffusion}, discarding the time-dependent weights yields the highly successful $\mathcal{L}_{simple}$ objective used in modern implementations.

\subsection{Generalized Sampling: From DDPM to DDIM}
\label{app:diffusion_sampling}

\autoref{sec:bg_diffusion} introduces the DDPM and DDIM samplers conceptually; here we derive the generalized reverse step of \textcite{song_denoising_2022} from which both follow as special cases.

\textcite{song_denoising_2022} replace the Markovian DDPM forward chain with a family of inference processes $q_\sigma(x_{1:T}|x_0) = q_\sigma(x_T|x_0)\prod_{t=2}^{T} q_\sigma(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0)$ indexed by $\sigma \in \mathbb{R}^T_{\ge 0}$. Each reverse-conditional $q_\sigma(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0)$ is conditioned on the clean sample $x_0$; applying Bayes' rule, the implied forward step $q_\sigma(x_t|x_{t-1}, x_0)$ therefore also depends on $x_0$, so each $x_t$ depends on both $x_{t-1}$ and $x_0$. This is what makes the process \emph{non-Markovian}, in contrast to the DDPM forward chain of \autoref{app:diffusion_forward}.

Concretely, the reverse-conditional is taken to be the Gaussian
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_reverse_conditional}
    q_\sigma(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\left( x_{t-1};\; \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}-\sigma_t^2}\;\frac{x_t - \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}\,x_0}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}},\; \sigma_t^2 I \right).
\end{equation}
Its mean is engineered (their Lemma~1) so that the marginals remain \emph{identical} to those of \acs{DDPM}, $q_\sigma(x_t|x_0) = \mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}\,x_0, (1-\overline{\alpha}_t) I)$ for every $\sigma$, which is precisely why the network $\epsilon_\theta$ trained under the \acs{DDPM} objective can be reused unchanged.

At inference time the clean sample $x_0$ is unavailable. Given $x_t$, the trained network predicts the noise and inverts \autoref{eq:forward_reparam} to obtain an estimate of $x_0$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_predicted_x0}
    f_\theta(x_t, t) := \frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}},
\end{equation}
and the generative process samples $x_{t-1}$ from the reverse-conditional with $x_0$ replaced by this estimate, $p_\theta(x_{t-1}|x_t) = q_\sigma\big(x_{t-1} \,\big|\, x_t, f_\theta(x_t,t)\big)$. This substitution simplifies neatly: plugging $x_0 = f_\theta(x_t,t)$ into the normalized residual of \autoref{eq:app_reverse_conditional} gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_residual_collapse}
    \frac{x_t - \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}\,f_\theta(x_t,t)}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}}
    = \frac{x_t - \big(x_t - \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\big)}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}}
    = \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t),
\end{equation}
so the residual collapses exactly to the predicted noise. Writing the resulting Gaussian as a reparameterized sample (its mean plus $\sigma_t\omega$) therefore yields the generalized reverse step
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_generalized_sampling}
\begin{aligned}
    x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \underbrace{\left( \frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}} \right)}_{\text{predicted } x_0 \,=\, f_\theta(x_t,t)}
    &+ \underbrace{\sqrt{1 - \overline{\alpha}_{t-1} - \sigma_t^2}\; \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)}_{\text{direction pointing to } x_t} \\
    &+ \underbrace{\sigma_t \omega}_{\text{random noise}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\omega \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and $\sigma_t \ge 0$ controls the injected stochasticity. The two deterministic terms are now transparent: $\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}\,f_\theta(x_t,t)$ is the rescaled prediction of $x_0$, and the second term steps back toward $x_t$ along the predicted-noise direction, with magnitude set by $\sigma_t$.

The choice of $\sigma_t$ recovers the two samplers of interest:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DDPM:} Setting $\sigma_t = \sqrt{(1 - \overline{\alpha}_{t-1}) / (1 - \overline{\alpha}_t)} \, \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t} = \sqrt{\tilde{\beta}_t}$ makes the forward process Markovian and recovers the original ancestral sampler of DDPM \parencite{ho_denoising_2020, song_denoising_2022}, with $\tilde{\beta}_t$ the forward-posterior variance of \autoref{app:diffusion_kl}.
    \item \textbf{DDIM:} Setting the variance to zero ($\sigma_t = 0$) removes the noise term $\sigma_t \omega$ and lets the square-root coefficient simplify to $\sqrt{1 - \overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}$, leaving the purely deterministic update
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:app_ddim_deterministic}
        x_{t-1} = \sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \left( \frac{x_t - \sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_t}\,\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_t}} \right) + \sqrt{1 - \overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}\, \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t).
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}
